## The claim

Narrowly: adequate sleep reliably improves mood, attention and emotional regulation over days-to-weeks — that part is experimental — and adolescent late sleep timing is biology, not laziness. The long-term "sleep saves your life" claim is much weaker, and we do not lean on it.

## The evidence

- **Adolescent circadian phase delay is biology, not laziness** (Carskadon and colleagues) — melatonin onset shifts later through puberty. A teenager who cannot sleep at 10pm is not being difficult; they are being 15. This single fact buys more goodwill than anything else in the talk.
- **Consensus: 8–10 hours** per night for 13–18-year-olds (American Academy of Sleep Medicine / Sleep Research Society).
- **Dunster et al., Sci Adv 2018;4:eaau6200** — Seattle delayed high-school start times by 55 minutes; wrist-actigraphy-measured sleep increased by **34 minutes**, with improved grades and attendance. A natural experiment with objective measurement — rare and valuable here.
- Short-term experimental sleep restriction reliably degrades attention, mood and emotional regulation (a large randomised-experimental literature). This is the immediately relevant part for a teenage audience.

## The honest weakness

Be candid: the long-term observational literature — the U-shaped associations between sleep duration and mortality — is badly confounded by reverse causation, because illness both shortens and lengthens sleep. There is no randomised trial of lifetime sleep duration and there never will be. Claims that sleep loss "causes" Alzheimer's disease in humans outrun the evidence. Sell sleep on the two weeks, not the fifty years — which is why this page's `clock` is set to *ten-year*.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

The honest pitch is not "sleep or you'll die young" — it is that sleep is the best-evidenced short-term lever on how you actually feel: your mood, your attention, your ability to handle a bad day. And the timing problem is not a character flaw. If you cannot fall asleep early, that is your biology; the fixable part is protecting the hours you can get, not blaming yourself for the ones you can't.

## The slogan to attack

There is no single industry counterpart to name here — the antagonists are diffuse: the attention economy that keeps a phone bright at midnight, and school start times set for adults' convenience rather than adolescent biology. The Seattle result is the rebuttal: change the *environment* (a later bell) and the sleep appears, without asking any teenager to try harder.
