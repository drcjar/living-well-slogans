## The claim

Narrowly: regular sunscreen use reduces skin cancer, and — unusually for a lifestyle exposure — this rests on a genuine randomised controlled trial rather than observation. And the slogan itself is a model of the form: three verbs, an action for each, no threat and no blame.

## The evidence

- **The Nambour Skin Cancer Prevention Trial** (Queensland, 1992–96) — 1,621 randomly selected adult residents, 2×2 factorial design, randomised to daily sunscreen versus discretionary use, and beta-carotene versus placebo. A genuine population-based randomised trial of a lifestyle exposure, which barely exists elsewhere.
  - **Green et al., Lancet 1999;354:723** — daily sunscreen reduced squamous cell carcinoma; no significant effect on basal cell carcinoma.
  - **Green et al., J Clin Oncol 2011;29:257** — ten years *after the intervention ceased*: **11 melanomas** in the daily-sunscreen arm versus **22** in the discretionary arm (HR **0.50**, 95% CI 0.24–1.02). For invasive melanoma: **3 versus 11** (HR **0.27**, 95% CI 0.08–0.97). The effect persisted a decade after the sunscreen stopped being applied.
- **Why it matters to teenagers specifically**: melanoma is one of the commonest cancers in 15–34-year-olds in the UK — one of the very few items where the ten-year and seventy-year clocks point the same way. Sunbeds are IARC Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans).
- **Slip, slop, slap (Australia, 1981)** is also the best *slogan* on the list: three verbs, an imitable action for each, no threat, no blame, naming the behaviour rather than the outcome. Melanoma incidence has since fallen in younger Australian birth cohorts while continuing to rise in older ones — consistent with a cohort effect from the campaign era.

## The honest weakness

One trial, one population (fair-skinned residents of high-UV Queensland), and melanoma was a *secondary* endpoint whose confidence interval for melanoma overall touches 1.0. The invasive-melanoma result rests on small numbers — **3 versus 11** cases. It is the best design available on this whole site, and the estimate is *still* imprecise — which is itself a useful thing to show a school audience about what real science looks like: even the best study here does not deliver certainty.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

This is one of the rare items where both clocks agree: melanoma is already among the commonest cancers in your age band, not a seventy-year-away abstraction. And the action is genuinely trivial — sunscreen, a shirt, shade, and staying off sunbeds (which are a Group 1 carcinogen, the same category as tobacco). Low effort, evidence from an actual trial, relevant now.

## The slogan to attack

There is no industry counterpart to attack — instead the antagonist is the tanning industry, and the sunbed specifically, which sells the exposure the evidence warns against. "Slip, slop, slap" earns its place on the *worked* side of the spine: it is what a good public-health slogan looks like when it names an action instead of assigning blame.
