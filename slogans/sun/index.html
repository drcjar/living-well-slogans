Slip, slop, slap

The best study design on this entire list. One of the very few lifestyle claims supported by an actual randomised controlled trial.

Confidence
High
Clock
Both clocks

For ages 8–11

Slip on a shirt, slop on sunscreen, slap on a hat. Three little actions, one for each word — that is the whole thing, and it is a really good piece of advice.

Why is it good? Because it does not threaten you or blame you. It just tells you three easy things to do. And unlike a lot of health advice, this one was actually tested properly in a real experiment, and it worked: people who used sunscreen regularly got less skin cancer later on.

The nice part is how little effort it takes — a shirt, some sunscreen, a hat, and staying in the shade when the sun is strong. Small actions, real protection.

For ages 11–16

Most items on this site are about risks decades away. This one is different — it is already relevant to you. Skin cancer called melanoma is one of the commonest cancers in people aged 15–34 in the UK. So this is one of the rare cases where "now" and "in fifty years" point the same way.

Two things worth knowing:

  • The action is genuinely trivial — sunscreen, a shirt, shade — and it is backed by an actual randomised trial, which is rarer than you'd think for this kind of advice.
  • Sunbeds are a Group 1 carcinogen — the same top danger category as tobacco. A tan from a sunbed is selling you exactly the exposure the evidence warns against.

What you do now bends future risk in a clean way here: sun damage adds up over a lifetime, and in the trial the protection was still visible a decade after people stopped using the sunscreen. Small habit, lasting effect.

For ages 16+

Narrowly: regular sunscreen use reduces skin cancer, and — unusually for a lifestyle exposure — this rests on a genuine randomised controlled trial rather than observation. The line itself is a model of the form: three verbs, an action for each, no threat and no blame.

The Nambour trial randomised a whole population to daily versus discretionary sunscreen, and ten years after the intervention stopped the daily-sunscreen arm had roughly half the melanomas. That persistence — benefit outlasting the behaviour — is the striking part, and it is why this is the strongest design on the site.

How today bends future risk: melanoma is already among the commonest cancers in your age band, so this is one of the very few items where the ten-year and seventy-year clocks agree; and because damage accumulates, the protective habit pays both now and later. Be honest about the limits, though — it is one trial, one fair-skinned high-UV population, melanoma was a secondary endpoint, and the invasive-melanoma result rests on very small numbers. Even the best study here does not deliver certainty.

The evidence

  • The Nambour Skin Cancer Prevention Trial (Queensland, 1992–96) — 1,621 randomly selected adult residents, 2×2 factorial design, randomised to daily sunscreen versus discretionary use, and beta-carotene versus placebo. A genuine population-based randomised trial of a lifestyle exposure, which barely exists elsewhere.
    • Green et al., Lancet 1999;354:723 — daily sunscreen reduced squamous cell carcinoma; no significant effect on basal cell carcinoma.
    • Green et al., J Clin Oncol 2011;29:257 — ten years after the intervention ceased: 11 melanomas in the daily-sunscreen arm versus 22 in the discretionary arm (HR 0.50, 95% CI 0.24–1.02). For invasive melanoma: 3 versus 11 (HR 0.27, 95% CI 0.08–0.97). The effect persisted a decade after the sunscreen stopped being applied.
  • Why it matters to teenagers specifically: melanoma is one of the commonest cancers in 15–34-year-olds in the UK — one of the very few items where the ten-year and seventy-year clocks point the same way. Sunbeds are IARC Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans).
  • Slip, slop, slap (Australia, 1981) is also the best-formed public-health line of its kind: three verbs, an imitable action for each, no threat, no blame, naming the behaviour rather than the outcome. Melanoma incidence has since fallen in younger Australian birth cohorts while continuing to rise in older ones — consistent with a cohort effect from the campaign era.

The honest weakness

One trial, one population (fair-skinned residents of high-UV Queensland), and melanoma was a secondary endpoint whose confidence interval for melanoma overall touches 1.0. The invasive-melanoma result rests on small numbers — 3 versus 11 cases. It is the best study design here, and the estimate is still imprecise — a reminder that even the strongest evidence on the site does not deliver certainty.
