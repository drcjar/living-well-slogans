## For ages 8–11

Almost nothing you do at your age is likely to make you ill or hurt you badly — and when a child your age does get seriously ill, it is nearly always something nobody chose, like a rare illness or an accident. Smoking is not on that list yet. It is a **slow** danger, not a fast one.

So why talk about it now? Because of one strange fact: **almost everyone who ends up smoking for life started when they were young — around your age or a little older.** The people who sell cigarettes and vapes know this. That is why the danger is worth knowing about early, even though it feels far away.

Here is the good news, and it is the whole point: with smoking, **not starting fixes everything.** You do not have to be strong or give anything up. If you never start, the entire problem simply never happens to you. It is one of the very few big health choices where doing *nothing* is the winning move.

## For ages 11–16

If you look at what actually harms people your age in this country, smoking is not near the top. The things most likely to seriously hurt or kill a teenager are **accidents — especially on the roads — and self-harm.** Cigarettes belong to a much slower clock: they mostly harm people decades from now.

But your teens are exactly when that slow clock gets *started*, and it is exactly when nicotine gets sold to you — now mostly as vapes. So two honest things at once:

- **Vaping nicotine is not the same as smoking.** Cigarettes harm you by *burning* — the smoke is full of the chemicals that do the long-term damage. A vape does not burn, so it delivers far fewer of those chemicals. Anyone who tells you a vape is "just as bad as a cigarette" is wrong, and once you know that, you will stop trusting the rest of what they say. That is why being straight with you matters.
- **But for someone who has never smoked, starting to vape is pure loss.** You would be taking on an addiction with nothing to trade for it — vapes have only existed since around 2007, so nobody can honestly promise you they are safe over a lifetime, because that lifetime hasn't happened yet.

What you do now bends the whole rest of the line. **Not starting removes almost the entire future risk, at no cost to you.** It is the single highest-value health decision available to you — and the leverage is highest right now, precisely because this is when the addiction is designed to catch you.

And if someone you love already smokes, this matters for them too: quitting is genuinely hard, but it is **not** just willpower. Proper help — medicines plus support — makes success far more likely, and it is never too late. They do not have to do it alone.

## For ages 16+

Now you are old enough to weigh the long clock honestly, so here it is without softening.

Cigarettes are the most clearly proven cause of early death in all of medicine. Lifelong smokers die on average about **ten years younger** than people who never smoked, and roughly two in every three lifelong smokers are eventually killed by it. This is not a weak statistical hint; it is one of the most reproduced findings in science, shown in different countries, sexes and decades, with the biological mechanism traced all the way down to the exact DNA damage. The details and sources are in the evidence panel below — check them.

But the second half is what turns a grim fact into a decision you control:

- **The risk is not banked. It is largely reversible if you get out early.** Stopping before 40 avoids the large majority of the excess risk; stopping before 30 avoids almost all of it. Even stopping at 60 buys back years. There is no age at which quitting fails to help.
- **Initiation is the only part of the pathway fully in your hands.** Almost all lifelong smokers start before 20. Not starting removes the entire downstream problem at no cost — which is why, if you have made it this far without smoking, you have already made the highest-leverage move.
- **And if you already smoke, stopping is not a coin-flip of willpower.** Comprehensive support — behavioural help plus medication — and effective medicines (nicotine replacement, varenicline, cytisine) multiply the odds of success; the effect sizes are in the evidence below.

On vaping, the honest position is the same one as the younger band, just with the evidence attached: nicotine vaping delivers far fewer combustion toxicants than smoking and there is high-quality evidence it helps *established smokers* quit — for a smoker, switching is a genuine gain. For a never-smoker none of that applies: it is dependence with no offsetting benefit and no long-term safety record. (The 2019–20 lung-injury scare, EVALI, was vitamin E acetate in illicit THC cartridges, not nicotine vapes — getting that wrong is the fastest way to lose an argument you should win.)

## The evidence

### Clause 1 — smoking kills

- **Doll & Hill, [BMJ 1950;2:739](<https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739>)** — hospital case-control, 649 lung cancer cases; the association was already unmissable.
- **British Doctors Study — Doll, Peto, Boreham & Sutherland, [BMJ 2004;328:1519](<https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.38142.554479.AE>)** — 34,439 male doctors, 50 years of follow-up, near-complete ascertainment. Cigarette smokers died on average **about 10 years younger** than never-smokers.
- **Pirie et al., [Lancet 2013;381:133](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(12)61720-6>)** (Million Women Study) and **Jha et al., [NEJM 2013;368:341](<https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMsa1211128>)** (US NHIS) independently reproduce the ~10-year figure — different countries, sexes, eras.
- **Banks et al., [BMC Med 2015](<https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-015-0281-z>)** (Australian 45 and Up cohort) — roughly **two-thirds of lifelong smokers are killed by smoking**, an upward revision of the older "half" figure.
- **Mechanism is closed, not inferred**: Denissenko et al., [Science 1996;274:430](<https://doi.org/10.1126/science.274.5286.430>) showed benzo[a]pyrene diol epoxide forms adducts preferentially at the exact *TP53* codons (157, 248, 273) that are mutational hotspots in smokers' lung tumours. Exposure → adduct → mutation → tumour, at base-pair resolution.
- **Bradford Hill, [Proc R Soc Med 1965;58:295](<https://doi.org/10.1177/003591576505800503>)** — the viewpoints paper on causal inference was written *about this exposure*.

### Clause 2 — stopping helps

- **[Doll et al. 2004](<https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.38142.554479.AE>)**: cessation at 60, 50, 40 and 30 gained approximately **3, 6, 9 and 10 years** of life expectancy respectively.
- **[Pirie et al. 2013](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(12)61720-6>)**: stopping before 40 avoided **>90%** of the excess mortality; stopping before 30 avoided **>97%**. The risk is not banked — it is largely reversible if you get out early.

The second clause matters as much as the first: because the risk is largely reversible if you stop early, this is a reason to act, not a reason to give up.

### Stopping is achievable — support and treatment work

Quitting is hard, but it is not a test of character you simply pass or fail. What multiplies success is **comprehensive support** — behavioural help plus medication, as stop-smoking services provide — and effective medicines:

- **Combining medication with behavioural support** beats brief advice or going it alone — **[Stead et al., Cochrane 2016](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD008286.pub3>)** (RR **1.83**); adding behavioural support on top of medication helps further — **[Hartmann-Boyce et al., Cochrane 2019](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009670.pub4>)** (RR **1.15**).
- **Nicotine replacement therapy** (patches, gum, lozenges) raises quit rates by about half — **[Hartmann-Boyce et al., Cochrane 2018](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000146.pub5>)** (RR **1.55**).
- **Varenicline** more than doubles them, and oral **cytisine** also helps — **[Livingstone-Banks et al., Cochrane 2023](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD006103.pub8>)** (varenicline RR **2.32**; cytisine RR **1.30**).
- A network meta-analysis ranks **varenicline and combination NRT** highest among the drug options — **[Cahill et al., Cochrane 2013](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009329.pub2>)**.

### On vaping

- **[Cochrane (Lindson et al., 2024)](<https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010216.pub8>)**: high-certainty evidence that nicotine e-cigarettes help *established smokers* quit, better than nicotine-replacement therapy. For a smoker, switching is a gain; for a never-smoker the argument does not apply at all. Nicotine vaping delivers far lower levels of the combustion toxicants that cause the ~10-year deficit, and it is **not** equivalent to smoking — but 30-year safety data cannot exist for a product introduced around 2007.

## The honest weakness

There is essentially no honest weakness on the core claim: the evidence that smoking kills is about as strong as evidence in medicine gets, and the open questions are about policy, not about whether it is true. The one thing to keep straight is that the ~10-year figure is about *burning* tobacco — it does not carry over wholesale to vaping, which is a different and far newer thing.
