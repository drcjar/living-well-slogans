## For ages 8–11

This one is not really about luck — it is about **maths**, and once you see the maths you cannot un-see it.

A gambling game is built so that, on average, the people playing it lose and the company running it wins. That is not bad luck or a mean streak; it is a rule the company **decided in advance and wrote down.** They keep a small slice of everything that gets bet. You might win sometimes — that is what keeps it exciting — but play long enough and the slice always wins.

At your age the version to watch for is on a screen: games where you pay (or watch an advert) to open a "mystery box" or spin for a prize. They are training wheels for exactly this feeling. Knowing the trick — *the game was built so the maker comes out ahead* — is the whole defence. You do not have to be strong; you just have to see it.

## For ages 11–16

The most dangerous gambling products are the ones **on your phone**, and they are designed that way on purpose: fast, endless, always open, a new bet every few seconds. Speed and continuity are what make a product hard to stop, and plenty of people your age are already in the data.

Here is the unanswerable part, and why this one needs no complicated science:

- **The "house edge" is published.** For a game like roulette or an online slot, the company literally states, in the terms, what fraction of all the money bet it will keep. It is a guarantee, not a guess.
- So the honest line is: **you are not unlucky — you are on the wrong side of a number the company chose in advance, wrote down, and published.** Nobody has to cheat you. The edge is in the terms and conditions.

What you do now matters because the habit, and the harm, track the *product*, not your personality — the faster and more constant the game, the more people it hurts. The defence is not out-willpowering a well-built machine; it is declining to play a game whose maths you have now seen.

## For ages 16+

The arithmetic needs no p-value and is unanswerable: the negative expected value of a gambling product is a **published design parameter**, not an empirical finding. The operator chose it in advance and printed it in the terms. Play long enough and variance washes out; the edge does not.

On top of that sits a second, weaker, epidemiological claim — that the resulting harm is large and tracks the product, not the person — and it is graded honestly in the evidence panel: harm is measurably higher for the fastest, most continuous products (online slots) than for slower ones (sports betting), the same way tar delivery tracks the cigarette, not the smoker.

How today bends future risk: gambling spend shows a dose-responsive association with financial distress and higher subsequent mortality, and adolescents are already in the exposure data. The single most protective move is upstream — recognising that the machine's edge is fixed and published, and not opening the app in the first place. The causal claims about debt, relationship breakdown and suicide are real but confounded; the arithmetic is not confounded by anything.

## The evidence

### Tier 1 — mathematics, not statistics

No p-value required. The house edge is specified, not observed:

- Single-zero roulette: house edge **2.7%**. Double-zero roulette: **5.26%**. (Standard game specifications.)
- UK online slots: return-to-player is typically **92–96%** — the machine is *specified* to keep 4–8% of everything staked, and the operator publishes it.
- The National Lottery returns roughly **half** of stakes as prizes.

These are guarantees, not tendencies. Play long enough and variance washes out; the edge does not. **You are not unlucky — you are on the wrong side of a number the company chose in advance, wrote down, and published.** Nobody has to lie to you. The edge is in the terms and conditions.

### Tier 2 — the epidemiology

- **Lancet Public Health Commission on Gambling (Wardle et al., [Lancet Public Health 2024](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667(24)00167-1>))** — systematic review and meta-analysis, 299 prevalence studies. **46.2% of adults** and **17.9% of adolescents** gambled in the past year globally; **10.3% of adolescents** gambled online. Any-risk gambling affected **5.5% of women and 11.9% of men**, extrapolating to roughly **448.7 million adults** worldwide. Net consumer losses are projected near **US$700 billion by 2028**, with gambling legal in more than 80% of countries.
- **Risk is product-specific** (Wardle et al. 2024): gambling disorder in **15.8% of online casino/slot players** versus **8.9% of sports bettors**. This is the direct analogue of tar delivery — harm tracks the product design (speed, continuity, in-play betting), not the character of the player.
- **Muggleton et al., [Nat Hum Behav 2021;5:319](<https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-020-01045-w>)** — 6.5 million UK bank customers, transaction-level data. Gambling spend shows a dose-responsive association with financial distress and higher subsequent mortality. Not causal, but objective exposure measurement, which is rare in this field.
- **Karlsson & Håkansson, [J Behav Addict 2018](<https://doi.org/10.1556/2006.7.2018.112>)** (Swedish national registers) report roughly **15-fold** elevated suicide rates in gambling disorder. Confounded — but confounding does not usually manufacture magnitudes like that.

## The honest weakness

Causal identification for the downstream harms — debt, relationship breakdown, suicide — is poor. Deprivation and mental illness confound everything, in both directions. The best quasi-experimental support comes from policy shocks: Norway's 2007 removal of slot machines, and the UK's 2019 cut of the fixed-odds betting terminal maximum stake from £100 to £2. Note also what is deliberately *not* claimed here: any figure for the share of industry revenue that comes from harmed players — the concept is sound but the circulating numbers trace to advocacy sources.
