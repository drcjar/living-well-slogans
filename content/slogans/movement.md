## For ages 8–11

This is the cheerful one. You do not need a gym, and you do not need to hit any special number.

Here is the real finding, and it is good news: the **biggest** health boost from moving comes simply from *not being one of the people who barely moves at all.* Going from almost-no-movement to some-movement is where nearly all the benefit is. After that, more is a nice bonus, not a rule.

So whatever gets you off the sofa counts — running around, cycling, a sport, dancing, walking the dog. Getting up and moving *at all* is the win.

And the best bit for right now: moving your body actually makes you feel good *today* — a better mood, a clearer head, less grumpiness. You don't have to wait fifty years to get something out of it.

## For ages 11–16

The science here is genuinely encouraging:

- The single biggest gain on the whole curve is **moving out of the least-active group.** If you do almost nothing now, a little regular movement buys you more than a very-fit person gains by training even harder.
- The useful dose is **lower than most people think.** Most of the benefit arrives well before any big daily target.

So there is nothing you have to "hit" and no membership to buy. Whatever movement you can tolerate — a sport, a walk, a bike ride — captures most of what matters. What you build now is a habit that keeps paying off for decades, but even judged on today it is the most winnable item on this whole site.

And the payoff is not only decades away. Regular activity is one of the most reliable ways to lift a low mood and take the edge off stress and anxiety — something you feel in days, not decades. There is even genetic evidence that being more active *causes* lower rates of depression, not just that cheerful people happen to move more.

One concrete, free option worth knowing about: **parkrun** — a free, weekly, timed 5 km run *or walk* in a local park, open to anyone and deliberately welcoming to complete beginners (plenty of people walk it). It rolls the two benefits into one cheap package: movement, plus a bit of outdoor social contact. It is a neat example of the whole idea — not "try harder," but a low-barrier default that makes the good choice easy.

## For ages 16+

Narrowly: physical activity lowers mortality; the benefit is largest for people moving *out of the lowest* activity band; and the dose that captures most of it is far below the round numbers in circulation.

The dose-response is steeply inverse and **front-loaded** — the largest reduction in mortality comes from leaving the bottom quartile, measured with accelerometers so it isn't inflated by self-report. Getting off the floor matters far more than optimising the top.

How today bends future risk: the honest causal signal (reverse causation is the obvious worry — ill people move less) is supported by Mendelian-randomisation work using accelerometer-derived phenotypes, which points to a genuine protective effect. Do not oversell the top of the curve, where confounding is heavy; the claim is strongest exactly where it matters most for a population — at the bottom, where the effect is largest and the causal support best.

And the benefit is not only on the seventy-year clock. Physical activity is one of the best-evidenced near-term levers on mood, reliably easing symptoms of depression and anxiety over weeks — and a Mendelian-randomisation study supports a *causal* effect of activity on lower depression risk, since genotype is fixed at birth and cannot be explained away by "happier people move more." That near-term, causal wellbeing effect is why this page sits on both clocks.

## The evidence

- **WHO 2020 guidelines (Bull et al., [Br J Sports Med 2020;54:1451](<https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2020-102955>))** — 150–300 min/week of moderate activity for adults; 60 min/day for 5–17-year-olds. The 2020 revision removed the old 10-minute bout minimum: all movement counts.
- **Ekelund et al., [BMJ 2019;366:l4570](<https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.l4570>)** — harmonised meta-analysis of accelerometer-measured cohorts (~36,000 adults), so no self-report inflation. The dose-response is steeply inverse and front-loaded: the largest mortality reduction comes from moving out of the lowest activity quartile. Getting off the floor matters far more than optimising the top.
- **Paluch et al., [Lancet Public Health 2022;7:e219](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667(21)00302-9>)** — steps meta-analysis, ~47,000 adults. Most of the mortality benefit is captured at a daily step count well below the popular round numbers, and it then plateaus — more is not proportionally better.
- **Causality**: reverse causation is the obvious threat — ill people move less. Mendelian randomisation built on genetic instruments for physical activity (from the GWAS of Klimentidis et al., [Int J Obes 2018](<https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-018-0120-3>)) supports a genuine protective effect, materially strengthening the observational picture.
- **Mood and wellbeing**: **Choi et al., [JAMA Psychiatry 2019;76:399](<https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2018.4175>)** — a two-sample Mendelian-randomisation study finding that accelerometer-measured physical activity *causally* lowers the risk of major depression (the relationship also runs the other way, with depression reducing activity). This is unusually strong evidence for a near-term, felt-now benefit, alongside a large experimental literature showing activity improves mood and eases anxiety over weeks.
- **A concrete, free example — parkrun**: **Stevinson et al., [Int J Behav Med 2015;22:170](<https://doi.org/10.1007/s12529-014-9431-5>)** — a qualitative study of why parkrun works: its *freedom* (free, accessible, inclusive) and *reciprocity* (social support, helping others) drive both starting and sticking with it — a template for low-barrier community activity. Honest caveat — **Haake et al., [Health Promot Int 2022;37](<https://doi.org/10.1093/heapro/daab138>)**: uptake skews to more affluent areas (one prosperous Sheffield parkrun drew about seven times the participants of a deprived one), so simply providing it is necessary but not sufficient for equitable benefit.

## The honest weakness

The upper reaches of the curve are confounded beyond what adjustment fixes; people doing 300 min/week differ from the sedentary in ways questionnaires do not capture. The claim is strongest exactly where it matters most for a population — the bottom of the distribution — where the effect is largest and the Mendelian-randomisation support best. Do not oversell the top end. On mood: the causal evidence is real but was clearest for *objectively measured* activity, and the effect size is modest — a reliable lift, not a cure. And community options like parkrun are promising rather than proven — the evidence is observational and self-selected (the people who turn up are already more motivated), and uptake skews to more advantaged areas — so treat it as a good option to make available, not a guaranteed fix.
