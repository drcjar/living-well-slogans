## The claim

The negative expected value of a gambling product is not an empirical finding to be estimated — it is a **published design parameter** the operator chose in advance and printed in the terms. So the core claim needs no study at all. A second, weaker claim sits on top of it: that the resulting harm is large and tracks the *product*, not the personality. That part is epidemiology, and we grade it honestly.

## The evidence

### Tier 1 — mathematics, not statistics

No p-value required. The house edge is specified, not observed:

- Single-zero roulette: house edge **2.7%**. Double-zero roulette: **5.26%**. (Standard game specifications.)
- UK online slots: return-to-player is typically **92–96%** — the machine is *specified* to keep 4–8% of everything staked, and the operator publishes it.
- The National Lottery returns roughly **half** of stakes as prizes.

These are guarantees, not tendencies. Play long enough and variance washes out; the edge does not. Make the point with force: **you are not unlucky — you are on the wrong side of a number the company chose in advance, wrote down, and published.** Nobody has to lie to you. The edge is in the terms and conditions.

### Tier 2 — the epidemiology

- **Lancet Public Health Commission on Gambling (Wardle et al., Lancet Public Health 2024)** — systematic review and meta-analysis, 299 prevalence studies. **46.2% of adults** and **17.9% of adolescents** gambled in the past year globally; **10.3% of adolescents** gambled online. Any-risk gambling affected **5.5% of women and 11.9% of men**, extrapolating to roughly **448.7 million adults** worldwide. Net consumer losses are projected near **US$700 billion by 2028**, with gambling legal in more than 80% of countries.
- **Risk is product-specific** (Wardle et al. 2024): gambling disorder in **15.8% of online casino/slot players** versus **8.9% of sports bettors**. This is the direct analogue of tar delivery — harm tracks the product design (speed, continuity, in-play betting), not the character of the player.
- **Muggleton et al., Nat Hum Behav 2021;5:319** — 6.5 million UK bank customers, transaction-level data. Gambling spend shows a dose-responsive association with financial distress and higher subsequent mortality. Not causal, but objective exposure measurement, which is rare in this field.
- **Karlsson & Håkansson, J Behav Addict 2018** (Swedish national registers) report roughly **15-fold** elevated suicide rates in gambling disorder. Confounded — but confounding does not usually manufacture magnitudes like that.

## The honest weakness

Causal identification for the downstream harms — debt, relationship breakdown, suicide — is poor. Deprivation and mental illness confound everything, in both directions. The best quasi-experimental support comes from policy shocks: Norway's 2007 removal of slot machines, and the UK's 2019 cut of the fixed-odds betting terminal maximum stake from £100 to £2. Note also what is deliberately *not* claimed here: any figure for the share of industry revenue that comes from harmed players — the concept is sound but the circulating numbers trace to advocacy sources.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

The highest-risk products are the ones on your phone, designed for speed and continuity — and adolescents are already in the exposure data (**17.9%** gambled in the past year; **10.3%** online, per Wardle et al. 2024). The defence is not willpower against a well-designed machine; it is recognising that the machine's edge is fixed and published, and declining to play a game whose maths you have now seen.

## The slogan to attack

**"When the fun stops, stop"** (Senet Group / GambleAware), withdrawn in 2021 for "Take time to think". Experimental and content-analytic work (Newall and colleagues; van Schalkwyk et al.) found no reduction in gambling and, in some framings, a backfire. Two reasons, both instructive: it frames the product as fun-with-a-caveat, and it asks the gambler to detect the moment the fun stops — exactly the perception that fails in disordered gambling. Contrast **"Odds are, you'll lose"**, which makes no claim about the gambler at all — the same rhetorical move tobacco control made in going from "smoking may damage your health" to "smoking kills".
