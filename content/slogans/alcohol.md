## The claim

Narrowly: there is no dose of alcohol below which risk is zero, and the old "J-curve" showing protection at low doses was an artefact of how the studies were built. This is a claim about **monotonicity** — the curve rises from the origin — not about magnitude. The absolute excess risk of one drink a week is small, and the slogan is honest only if it says so.

## The evidence

- **Zhao, Stockwell, Naimi et al., JAMA Netw Open 2023;6:e236185** — 107 cohort studies, **4.8 million participants**. The apparent protection of low-volume drinking **disappeared** after adjustment for study characteristics, chiefly abstainer bias: reference "non-drinker" groups contaminated with sick-quitters and former heavy drinkers. No significant mortality reduction below 25 g/day; significantly increased mortality at **≥25 g/day in women and ≥45 g/day in men**. Apparent cardioprotection appeared only in older cohorts — precisely those most exposed to lifetime selection bias. (Corrected May 2023 for confidence-interval errors in the figure.)
- **Millwood et al., Lancet 2019;393:1831** (China Kadoorie Biobank, ~500,000 people) — the decisive study, and the one to lead with for a research audience. Using *ALDH2* rs671 and *ADH1B* rs1229984 as genetic instruments, conventional epidemiology showed the familiar U-shape for stroke; the genetic analysis showed a **monotonic linear increase**. Genotype is assigned at conception and cannot be confounded by social position or sick-quitting. The U-shape was confounding.
- **IARC (Monograph 100E)** classifies alcoholic beverages as Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans) since 1988, and acetaldehyde associated with alcohol consumption as Group 1 — causal for cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, oesophagus, liver, colorectum and female breast.
- **Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, Br J Cancer 2002** — breast cancer risk rises about **7% per 10 g/day** of alcohol, with no threshold. This is the strongest single support for "no safe dose": the curve rises from zero.
- **UK Chief Medical Officers 2016** guidance: no level of drinking is completely safe; keep to ≤14 units/week for both sexes. **Canada's Guidance on Alcohol and Health (CCSA 2023)** went further, abandoning thresholds for a continuum (1–2 drinks/week low risk, 3–6 moderate, 7+ increasingly high).

## The honest weakness

Say it out loud: "no safe dose" is a claim about monotonicity, not magnitude. There is also no *safe* dose of cycling, of sunlight, or of getting into a car. The absolute excess risk at one drink a week is very small, and an audience told otherwise will correctly conclude it is being managed. The defensible formulation keeps the truth and drops the overclaim: **"There is no dose at which alcohol is good for you. There never was. The risk starts at your first drink and rises with every one after it."**

## What this means for a 15-year-old

The lifetime cancer arithmetic is not your risk. **Your risk is tonight**: injury, assault, drowning, unconsciousness, and things done to you or by you that cannot be undone. Same-evening, high-magnitude, and far more relevant than cirrhosis three decades away. Lead with that.

## The slogan to attack

**"Drink responsibly."** Written by the seller. It implies a responsible dose exists while defining none, and pre-assigns blame for any harm to the drinker. It is the alcohol industry's exact analogue of gambling's "When the fun stops, stop" — a sentence whose function is to relocate responsibility onto the customer in advance.
