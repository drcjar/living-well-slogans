# Living Well: Slogans and the Evidence Behind Them
### Evidence pack for a short school talk
*Prepared July 2026. Effect sizes are given with the design that generated them, and with the honest weaknesses.*

---

## PAGE 0 — OVERVIEW AND PRIORITISATION

### The organising argument

The slogans that changed population health are **honest statements about a product or an environment**. The slogans that failed **put the burden on the individual's willpower**.

| Worked | Failed / weak |
|---|---|
| Smoking kills | Just say no (DARE) |
| Back to sleep | Drink responsibly |
| Slip, slop, slap | When the fun stops, stop |
| Clunk click, every trip | 10,000 steps |

The first column describes the world. The second column describes *you*, and implies that if you are harmed it is because you failed. Notice who wrote each column: the left is public health; much of the right is the industry selling the product, or a marketing department.

**This is the spine of the talk.** Living well is much less about being strong than about (a) knowing which products are engineered against you, and (b) arranging life so the good choice is the default choice.

### Prioritisation — two different clocks

An honest epidemiologist has to concede that the standard "living well" slogans are almost all about the **70-year clock**, not the **10-year clock**. For a 14-year-old in England, the leading causes of death over the next decade are suicide and self-harm, injury (chiefly road), and cancer — not lung cancer, cirrhosis or ischaemic heart disease. Deaths in this age group are rare in absolute terms; but their distribution is not what the slogans address. Two lists, and the talk should say so explicitly.

**Priority on the ten-year clock (acute, salient, actionable now)**
1. **Don't start nicotine.** Almost all lifetime smokers start before 20. The highest-leverage decision available to a 14-year-old, because *initiation* is the only part of the pathway fully under their control.
2. **Alcohol and acute risk** — injury, assault, drowning, poisoning. Not cirrhosis.
3. **Road** — seatbelt, helmet, and the passenger-of-a-young-driver problem.
4. **Gambling** — 17.9% of adolescents gambled in the past year globally; the highest-risk products are on their phone.
5. **Sleep and mood** — the best-evidenced short-term lever on how they actually feel.

**Priority on the seventy-year clock (magnitude × strength of causal evidence)**
1. **Smoking** — ~10 years of life; evidence class: near-experimental, dose-responsive, mechanistically closed.
2. **Physical activity / sedentary time** — large, steepest at the bottom of the distribution; strong device-measured observational data plus supportive Mendelian randomisation.
3. **Alcohol** — large at high doses; evidence *improved* by genetic instruments that remove abstainer bias.
4. **Diet / food environment** — very large attributable burden, **weakest evidence base per slogan** (Page 4). Be honest about this in front of a science-literate audience.
5. **Gambling** — unimpeachable arithmetic (house edge) + growing epidemiology + weak causal identification for downstream harms.
6. **Sun protection** — small in mortality terms, but the **best study design on this list** (an actual RCT), and melanoma is one of the commonest cancers in 15–34s.
7. **Sleep** — good short-term evidence, poor long-term causal evidence.

### The final slogan set

> **Smoking kills — stopping helps.**
> **Alcohol: no safe dose.**
> **Gambling: designed so you lose.**
> **Move more, sit less.**
> **Eat food. Mostly plants. Not too much.**
> **Sleep like it matters.**
> **Slip, slop, slap.**
> **And: anyone who tells you to enjoy their product "responsibly" is telling you whose fault it will be.**

## PAGE 1 — "SMOKING KILLS, STOPPING HELPS"

**Verdict: the strongest causal claim in non-communicable disease epidemiology. Both clauses independently evidenced.**

### Clause 1: smoking kills

- **Doll & Hill, BMJ 1950;2:739** — hospital case-control, 649 lung cancer cases; the association was already unmissable.
- **British Doctors Study — Doll, Peto, Boreham & Sutherland, BMJ 2004;328:1519** — 34,439 male doctors, 50 years of follow-up, near-complete ascertainment. Cigarette smokers died on average **about 10 years younger** than never-smokers.
- **Pirie et al., Lancet 2013;381:133** (Million Women Study) and **Jha et al., NEJM 2013;368:341** (US NHIS) independently reproduce the ~10-year figure — different countries, sexes, eras.
- **Roughly two-thirds of lifelong smokers are killed by smoking** (Banks et al., BMC Med 2015, Australian 45 and Up cohort) — an upward revision of the older "half" figure.
- **Mechanism is closed, not inferred**: Denissenko et al., Science 1996;274:430 showed benzo[a]pyrene diol epoxide forms adducts preferentially at the exact *TP53* codons (157, 248, 273) that are mutational hotspots in smokers' lung tumours. Exposure → adduct → mutation → tumour, at base-pair resolution.
- **Bradford Hill, Proc R Soc Med 1965;58:295** — the viewpoints paper was written *about this exposure*.

### Clause 2: stopping helps

This is the clause that makes it an intervention rather than an epitaph.

- **Doll 2004**: cessation at 60, 50, 40 and 30 gained approximately **3, 6, 9 and 10 years** of life expectancy.
- **Pirie 2013**: stopping before 40 avoided **>90%** of excess mortality; before 30, **>97%**. The risk is not banked — it is largely reversible if you get out early.
- Note the structure: a threat **plus an exit**. Threat without exit produces fatalism. This is why "smoking kills" alone is a worse slogan than the full line.

### For a 2026 audience: vaping

Be straight with them; they will know if you aren't.
- Nicotine vaping delivers far lower levels of the combustion toxicants that cause the ~10-year deficit. It is not equivalent to smoking, and pretending otherwise destroys your credibility for everything else in the talk.
- **Cochrane (Lindson et al., 2024)**: high-certainty evidence that nicotine e-cigarettes help *established smokers* quit, better than NRT. For a smoker, switching is a gain.
- For a never-smoker that argument does not apply at all: dependence acquired with no offsetting benefit, and 30-year safety data cannot exist for a product introduced around 2007.
- **EVALI (2019–20) was vitamin E acetate in illicit THC cartridges**, not nicotine vapes. Say so — getting this wrong is the fastest way to lose the room.

### Honest weakness
Essentially none on causation. The live arguments are about policy.

## PAGE 2 — "ALCOHOL: NO SAFE DOSE"

**Verdict: defensible, but it is a claim about the *shape* of a curve, not the *size* of a risk. Say both parts or they'll catch you.**

### The evidence that the J-curve was an artefact

- **Zhao, Stockwell, Naimi et al., JAMA Netw Open 2023;6:e236185** — 107 cohort studies, **4.8 million participants**. The apparent protection of low-volume drinking **disappeared** after adjustment for study characteristics, chiefly **abstainer bias**: reference "non-drinker" groups contaminated with sick-quitters and former heavy drinkers. No significant mortality reduction below 25 g/day. Significantly increased mortality at **≥25 g/day in women, ≥45 g/day in men**. Apparent cardioprotection appeared only in older cohorts — precisely those most exposed to lifetime selection bias. (Corrected May 2023 for CI errors in the figure.)
- **Millwood et al., Lancet 2019;393:1831** (China Kadoorie Biobank, ~500,000) — the decisive study, and the one to lead with for a research audience. Using *ALDH2* rs671 and *ADH1B* rs1229984 as instruments, conventional epidemiology showed the familiar U-shape for stroke; the **genetic analysis showed a monotonic linear increase**. Genotype is assigned at conception and cannot be confounded by social position or sick-quitting. The U-shape was confounding.
- **IARC**: alcoholic beverages Group 1 since 1988; acetaldehyde associated with alcohol consumption Group 1 (Monograph 100E). Causal for oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, oesophagus, liver, colorectum, female breast.
- **Breast cancer has no threshold**: ~7% increase per 10 g/day (Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, Br J Cancer 2002). This is the strongest single support for "no safe dose" — the curve rises from zero.
- **Canada's Guidance on Alcohol and Health (CCSA 2023)** abandoned thresholds for a continuum: 1–2 drinks/week low risk, 3–6 moderate, 7+ increasingly high. **UK CMO 2016**: no level completely safe; ≤14 units/week, both sexes.

### The honest weakness — say it out loud

"No safe dose" is a claim about **monotonicity**, not magnitude. There is also no safe dose of cycling, of sunlight, or of getting into a car. The absolute excess risk at one drink a week is very small, and an audience told otherwise will correctly conclude it is being managed. **The defensible formulation: "There is no dose at which alcohol is good for you. There never was. The risk starts at your first drink and rises with every one after it."** True, and it does not overclaim.

### What actually matters for a 15-year-old
The lifetime cancer arithmetic is not their risk. Their risk is tonight: injury, assault, drowning, unconsciousness, and things done to them or by them that cannot be undone. Same-evening, high-magnitude. Lead with that.

### The slogan to attack
**"Drink responsibly."** Written by the seller. Implies a responsible dose exists while defining none, and pre-assigns blame for any harm to the drinker. It is the alcohol industry's "When the fun stops, stop."

## PAGE 3 — "GAMBLING: DESIGNED SO YOU LOSE"

**Verdict: the only slogan here that needs no epidemiology to be true. It is arithmetic. Lead with that.**

### Tier 1: mathematics, not statistics

No p-value required. The negative expected value is a **published design parameter of the product**:
- Single-zero roulette: house edge **2.7%**. Double-zero: **5.26%**.
- UK online slots: return-to-player typically **92–96%** — the machine is *specified* to keep 4–8% of everything staked, and the operator publishes this.
- National Lottery: roughly **half** the stake returns as prizes.
- These are guarantees, not tendencies. Play long enough, variance washes out, the edge does not.

Make this point with force: **you are not unlucky. You are on the correct side of a number the company chose in advance, wrote down, and published.** Nobody has to lie to you. The edge is in the terms and conditions.

### Tier 2: the epidemiology

**Lancet Public Health Commission on Gambling (Wardle et al., Lancet Public Health 2024)** — systematic review and meta-analysis, 299 prevalence studies:
- **46.2% of adults** and **17.9% of adolescents** gambled in the past year globally; **10.3% of adolescents gambled online**.
- Any-risk gambling in **5.5% of women and 11.9% of men** → extrapolated, **~448.7 million adults** affected worldwide; on the order of 80 million meeting criteria for gambling disorder.
- **Risk is product-specific**: gambling disorder in **15.8% of online casino/slot players** vs **8.9% of sports bettors**. This is the direct analogue of tar delivery — harm tracks the *product design* (speed, continuity, in-play betting), not the personality.
- Net consumer losses projected at nearly **US$700 billion by 2028**; gambling legal in **>80% of countries**.

Supporting:
- **Muggleton et al., Nat Hum Behav 2021;5:319** — 6.5 million UK bank customers, transaction-level data. Gambling spend shows a dose-responsive association with financial distress and higher subsequent mortality. Not causal, but objective exposure measurement, which is rare here.
- Swedish national register data (Karlsson & Håkansson, J Behav Addict 2018) report roughly **15-fold** elevated suicide rates in gambling disorder. Confounded — but confounding does not usually manufacture magnitudes like that.

### The failed slogan — a case study for the talk

**"When the fun stops, stop"** (Senet Group / GambleAware), withdrawn 2021 for "Take time to think". Experimental and content-analytic work (Newall and colleagues; van Schalkwyk et al.) found no reduction in gambling and, in some framings, a backfire. Two reasons, both instructive:
1. It frames the product as **fun, with a caveat**.
2. It asks the gambler to detect the moment the fun stops — precisely the perception that has failed in disordered gambling.

"Odds are, you'll lose" makes no claim about the gambler at all. It is a statement about the product — the same rhetorical move tobacco control made going from "smoking may damage your health" to "smoking kills".

### Honest weakness
Causal identification for downstream harms (debt, relationship breakdown, suicide) is poor — deprivation and mental illness confound everything, in both directions. Best quasi-experimental support: Norway's 2007 removal of slot machines, and the UK's 2019 cut of the FOBT maximum stake from £100 to £2.

## PAGE 4 — "EAT LESS, MOVE MORE" → **"EAT FOOD. MOSTLY PLANTS. NOT TOO MUCH."**

**Verdict: "eat less, move more" is thermodynamically true and behaviourally near-useless. It is the "just say no" of obesity. Retire it, and explain why.**

### Why the slogan fails

It is an identity, not an instruction — true in the way "spend less than you earn" is true. It contains no information about *why* intake is high, and silently locates the cause in the eater's character. The evidence that this is the wrong causal model:

- **Hall et al., Cell Metab 2019;30:67** — the best-controlled feeding experiment in nutrition. Inpatient randomised crossover, 20 adults, ultra-processed vs unprocessed diets **matched for calories, sugar, fat, fibre and macronutrients**, eaten *ad libitum*. On the ultra-processed diet people spontaneously ate **~500 kcal/day more** and gained ~0.9 kg in two weeks; they lost it on the unprocessed diet. **The amount eaten was a property of the food, not of the person.** The whole argument in one trial.
- **Hall et al., Obesity 2016;24:1612** (the "Biggest Loser" follow-up) — six years after extreme weight loss, resting metabolic rate remained suppressed beyond what weight loss predicted, and 13 of 14 regained. The body defends its mass. "Eat less" is advice to fight a homeostatic system with conscious attention.
- The RCT record for diet composition is genuinely poor: **Women's Health Initiative** low-fat, null for CVD and breast cancer; **Look AHEAD** (NEJM 2013), sustained weight loss in type 2 diabetes and **null for cardiovascular events**; **PREDIMED** (Estruch et al., NEJM 2018) is the strongest positive trial (~30% relative reduction in major CV events) and had to be retracted and republished after randomisation irregularities.

### What survives honestly

- **GBD 2017 Diet Collaborators, Lancet 2019;393:1958** — ~11 million deaths/year attributable to dietary risks, dominated by **high sodium, low whole grains, low fruit**. The top three are about what is *absent or added*, not about total calories.
- **Pollan's rule** outperforms "eat less, move more" because it is a rule about *what to buy* — about the environment — and only the third clause concerns restraint.
- **"5 a day" is a marketing number, not an evidence threshold** — chosen in a 1991 US campaign for achievability. Dose-response meta-analysis (Aune et al., Int J Epidemiol 2017) suggests benefit continuing to ~800 g/day, i.e. ten portions. A clean, non-cynical example of a number chosen for tractability and then mistaken for a finding.

### Honest weakness — state it plainly
Nutritional epidemiology is the weakest field on this page: self-reported food frequency questionnaires, massive residual confounding by socioeconomic position, and a replication record that would embarrass any other discipline. The Hall crossover is the exception, and it is small (n=20, 4 weeks). Anything stronger than "the food environment is engineered for overconsumption, and this has been shown experimentally once, well" is overclaiming.

## PAGE 5 — "MOVE MORE, SIT LESS"

**Verdict: large effect, steepest at the bottom, and the dose is far lower than anyone tells you. The most encouraging slide in the talk.**

- **WHO 2020 guidelines** (Bull et al., Br J Sports Med 2020;54:1451): 150–300 min/week moderate activity for adults; **60 min/day** for 5–17-year-olds. The 2020 revision **removed the 10-minute bout minimum** — all movement counts.
- **Ekelund et al., BMJ 2019;366:l4570** — harmonised meta-analysis of **accelerometer-measured** cohorts (~36,000 adults), so no self-report inflation. The dose-response is steeply inverse and **front-loaded**: the largest mortality reduction comes from moving out of the lowest activity quartile. Getting off the floor matters far more than optimising the top.
- **Paluch et al., Lancet Public Health 2022;7:e219** — steps meta-analysis, ~47,000 adults. Mortality falls up to roughly **6,000–8,000 steps/day in over-60s** and **8,000–10,000 in under-60s**, then **plateaus**.
- **10,000 steps has no evidential parentage at all**: it comes from *manpo-kei* ("10,000-step meter"), the brand name of a pedometer sold by Yamasa around the 1964 Tokyo Olympics — chosen partly because the character 万 (10,000) resembles a walking man. **Use this.** It is the friendliest possible demonstration that a number can circulate for sixty years as health advice without ever having been a finding.
- **Causality**: reverse causation is the obvious threat (ill people move less). Mendelian randomisation using accelerometer-derived phenotypes (Klimentidis et al., Eur Heart J 2018) supports a genuine protective effect, materially strengthening the observational picture.

### Honest weakness
The upper reaches of the curve are confounded beyond what adjustment fixes; people doing 300 min/week differ in ways questionnaires don't capture. The bottom of the curve — the part that matters for a population — is where the evidence is strongest and MR support best.

## PAGE 6 — "SLEEP LIKE IT MATTERS"

**Verdict: excellent short-term evidence, weak long-term causal evidence. Sell it as the lever on how you feel this month, not on how long you live.**

- **Adolescent circadian phase delay is biology, not laziness** (Carskadon and colleagues) — melatonin onset shifts later through puberty. A teenager who cannot sleep at 10pm is not being difficult; they are being 15. This single fact buys more goodwill than anything else in the talk.
- **Consensus: 8–10 hours** for 13–18-year-olds (AASM / Sleep Research Society).
- **Dunster et al., Sci Adv 2018;4:eaau6200** — Seattle delayed high-school start times by 55 minutes; **wrist-actigraphy-measured** sleep increased by **34 minutes**, with improved grades and attendance. A natural experiment with objective measurement — rare and valuable here.
- Short-term experimental sleep restriction reliably degrades attention, mood and emotional regulation. RCT-grade, and immediately relevant to them.

### Honest weakness — be candid
The long-term observational literature (U-shaped sleep-duration/mortality associations) is badly confounded by reverse causation: illness both shortens and lengthens sleep. There is no RCT of lifetime sleep duration and there never will be. Claims that sleep loss "causes" Alzheimer's disease in humans outrun the evidence. Sell sleep on the two weeks, not the fifty years.

## PAGE 7 — "SLIP, SLOP, SLAP"

**Verdict: the best study design on this entire list. One of the very few lifestyle claims supported by an actual RCT.**

- **The Nambour Skin Cancer Prevention Trial** (Queensland, 1992–96) — 1,621 randomly selected adult residents, 2×2 factorial, randomised to **daily sunscreen vs discretionary use** and beta-carotene vs placebo. A genuine population-based randomised trial of a lifestyle exposure.
  - **Green et al., Lancet 1999;354:723** — daily sunscreen reduced squamous cell carcinoma; no significant effect on basal cell carcinoma.
  - **Green et al., J Clin Oncol 2011;29:257** — ten years *after the intervention ceased*: **11 melanomas in the daily-sunscreen arm vs 22 in the discretionary arm** (HR **0.50**, 95% CI 0.24–1.02). For **invasive** melanoma: **3 vs 11** (HR **0.27**, 95% CI 0.08–0.97). Effect persisted a decade after the intervention stopped.
- **Why it matters to teenagers specifically**: melanoma is one of the commonest cancers in 15–34-year-olds in the UK — one of the very few items where the 10-year and 70-year clocks point the same way. Sunbeds are IARC Group 1.
- **Slip, slop, slap (Australia, 1981)** is also the best *slogan* on the list: three verbs, an action for each, imitable, no threat, no blame, and it names the behaviour rather than the outcome. Melanoma incidence has since fallen in younger Australian birth cohorts while continuing to rise in older ones — consistent with a cohort effect from the campaign era.

### Honest weakness
One trial, one population (fair-skinned, high-UV Queensland), and melanoma was a secondary endpoint whose CI for melanoma overall touches 1.0. The invasive result rests on small numbers (3 vs 11). It is the best design available and the estimate is *still* imprecise — which is itself a good thing to show a school audience about what science actually looks like.

## PAGE 8 — THE SLOGANS THAT FAILED (the closing slide)

**Verdict: the most valuable page in the pack, because it is the one they haven't heard.**

- **DARE / "Just say no"** — **Ennett et al., Am J Public Health 1994;84:1394** (meta-analysis) and **West & O'Neal, Am J Public Health 2004;94:1027** found effects on *knowledge* and essentially **none on drug use**. Some prevention programmes show iatrogenic effects. The most heavily funded school drug-education programme in history did not work.
- **"When the fun stops, stop"** — withdrawn 2021; no evidence of benefit, signal of backfire.
- **"Drink responsibly"** — no dose defined, written by the seller, blame pre-assigned.
- **"10,000 steps"** — a 1965 pedometer brand name.
- **"5 a day"** — chosen for achievability, not derived from a dose-response curve.

### What did work, and it wasn't education

Adult smoking prevalence in Great Britain fell from roughly **45% in the mid-1970s to about 11–12% now**. Tobacco control modelling (SimSmoke and similar) attributes the great majority of that decline to **taxation, advertising bans, smokefree legislation, plain packaging and age-of-sale law** — structural measures — and comparatively little to school health education. Seatbelt wearing did not rise because of "Clunk click"; it rose because of the 1983 law, which was associated with roughly a 25% reduction in driver deaths. **"Back to sleep" is the honourable outlier**: a pure information campaign that halved SIDS — and it worked because it asked for **one action, once, from a motivated actor, with no commercial product opposing it**. That is the exception that defines the rule.

### The conclusion to give them

The point of this talk is **not** to make you resolve to be better. Resolutions have a poor evidence base.

The point is that some very clever, very well-funded people have designed products — cigarettes, drinks, slot apps, snack foods, feeds — to be difficult to stop using, and have then written slogans telling you that stopping is your responsibility.

You cannot out-willpower a design. But you can **see** the design. And once you can see it, you get to choose your own defaults instead of having them chosen for you: what's in the cupboard, what's on your phone, who you're with, what time you go to bed.

> **Smoking kills — stopping helps.**
> **Alcohol: no safe dose.**
> **Gambling: designed so you lose.**
> **Move more, sit less. Sleep like it matters. Slip, slop, slap.**
> **And when someone tells you to enjoy their product responsibly — ask who benefits from that sentence.**
