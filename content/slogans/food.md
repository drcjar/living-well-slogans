## For ages 8–11

Carrying a lot of extra body fat is genuinely bad for you. As you get older it makes serious illnesses — diabetes, heart disease, even some cancers — much more likely. That is not about how you look; it is about what the extra fat does inside your body.

Here is the hard part, and the reason grown-ups make such a fuss about it early: **once your body gets used to carrying extra weight, it fights to keep it.** Losing it later and staying that way turns out to be really difficult for almost everyone. So by far the easiest time to deal with it is to *not get there in the first place*.

The good news is that this is much less about willpower than it sounds. **You eat more when more food is easy to reach.** If there is a bowl of crisps next to you, you eat crisps; if there is a bowl of apples, you eat apples. So the trick is not fighting yourself at the table — it is what ends up in the cupboard, which you and your family can decide, calmly, at the shop. That is what "eat food, mostly plants, not too much" is really about.

## For ages 11–16

Two blunt facts first, because they are the point:

- **Excess body fat causes disease.** It is not just a look — higher body fat *causally* raises your risk of type 2 diabetes, heart disease and several cancers, and shortens life on average. This is one of the better-evidenced things on the whole site (genetic studies back it up, so it is not just correlation).
- **The biggest risk factor for carrying excess weight later is carrying it now.** Weight tracks hard from childhood into adulthood, and once the body has gained fat it *defends* it — after weight loss, metabolism drops and the weight tends to come back. Reversal is genuinely hard; prevention is far easier.

Put those together and the useful move is obvious: **don't get there in the first place** — and that is far more about your environment than your willpower. The amount you eat is partly a property of the food itself: in the best experiment on this, the same people ate a lot more when the food was processed, without trying to. So the lever you actually control is upstream of hunger — what is in the kitchen and what is easiest to grab. Change the defaults and the eating changes with them.

## For ages 16+

Start with the part that is strong, because it is unusually strong for nutrition. **Excess adiposity causally raises the risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease** — Mendelian-randomisation studies, using genes fixed at birth, show this is causal rather than confounded. The IARC working group judged the evidence sufficient that excess body fatness **causes at least a dozen cancers**. And all-cause mortality rises with BMI above the mid-20s. So "why it matters" is not in doubt.

The other strong, and under-appreciated, fact is about **reversibility**: the body defends a fat mass once it is reached. After substantial weight loss, resting metabolic rate stays suppressed and the weight is usually regained; and obesity tracks powerfully from childhood — a child with obesity is roughly five times as likely to carry it into adulthood, and the great majority of adolescents with obesity stay that way. The practical upshot is stark: **the biggest risk factor for carrying excess weight is having carried it already.** Prevention beats reversal by a wide margin, which is why the leverage is early and environmental.

Where the evidence is *weak* is the next question — *which diet* to prescribe. Michael Pollan's *eat food, mostly plants, not too much* works less as a proven prescription than as a rule about **what to buy**: two of its three clauses are about the food environment, and the best-controlled feeding trial shows the quantity eaten is partly set by how processed the food is. So the honest position is: the stakes are high and well-evidenced; the *specific* dietary fix is not — change the defaults rather than trusting willpower or any single named diet.

## The evidence

### Why excess weight matters (the strong part)

- **Dale et al., [Circulation 2017;135:2373](<https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.116.026560>)** — Mendelian randomisation: higher genetically-predicted adiposity *causally* increases coronary heart disease, stroke and type 2 diabetes. Genes are fixed at birth, so this is causation, not confounding.
- **Lauby-Secretan et al. (IARC Working Group), [NEJM 2016;375:794](<https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27557308/>)** — sufficient evidence that the absence of excess body fatness lowers the risk of thirteen cancers; i.e. excess body fat causes them.
- **Global BMI Mortality Collaboration, [Lancet 2016;388:776](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(16)30175-1>)** — individual-participant meta-analysis, ~3.9 million adults: all-cause mortality is lowest around BMI 20–25 and rises with overweight and obesity (about **31%** higher per 5 kg/m² above 25).

### Why prevention beats reversal

- **Fothergill et al., [Obesity 2016;24:1612](<https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.21538>)** ("Biggest Loser" follow-up) — six years after major weight loss, resting metabolic rate remained suppressed beyond what weight loss alone predicted, and 13 of 14 participants regained. The body actively defends its mass.
- **Simmonds et al., [Obes Rev 2016;17:95](<https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.12334>)** — obesity tracks strongly from childhood: children with obesity are around five times as likely to have obesity as adults, and roughly **80%** of adolescents with obesity remain so into adulthood.

### What to eat (the weaker part)

- **Hall et al., [Cell Metab 2019;30:67](<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008>)** — the best-controlled feeding experiment in nutrition. Inpatient randomised crossover, 20 adults, ultra-processed versus unprocessed diets matched for calories, sugar, fat, fibre and macronutrients, eaten *ad libitum*. On the ultra-processed diet people spontaneously ate about **500 kcal/day** more and gained weight; they lost it on the unprocessed diet. The amount eaten was a property of the food, not of the person.
- **GBD 2017 Diet Collaborators, [Lancet 2019;393:1958](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(19)30041-8>)** — roughly **11 million deaths per year** attributable to dietary risks, dominated by high sodium, low whole grains and low fruit — the "mostly plants" clause.
- **Aune et al., [Int J Epidemiol 2017](<https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyw319>)** — the benefit of fruit and vegetables keeps rising to about **800 g/day**, roughly ten portions.
- **PREDIMED (Estruch et al., [NEJM 2018](<https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389>))** — the strongest positive dietary trial, a Mediterranean pattern with about a **30%** relative reduction in major cardiovascular events (retracted and republished after randomisation irregularities — strong, but not pristine).

## The honest weakness

Keep two things separate. That excess body fat *causes* serious disease is well-evidenced — genetic studies and the IARC assessment put it beyond the reach of the usual confounding complaints. What is weak is the *dietary prescription*: nutritional epidemiology leans on self-reported food-frequency questionnaires, is heavily confounded by socioeconomic position, and has a poor replication record. The Hall crossover is the honourable exception, and it is small (n=20, four weeks). So state the stakes plainly, but do not oversell any single named diet as the fix — which is why the page's confidence is only moderate.
