## For ages 8–11

Before any of the health pages, one rule for the whole site: **you are allowed to check.**

Grown-ups, adverts and posters will tell you lots of things about your health. Some are true, some are half-true, and some were written by a company that wants your money. The trick is not to believe everyone, and not to believe *no one* either — it is to ask a simple question: *how do you know that?*

Every page here tries to answer that question. It shows where each fact came from, how sure we are, and what is still shaky about it. If a page ever just tells you what to think without showing its homework, it has failed. **Knowing how to check is a kind of power** — it means nobody gets to decide the truth for you.

## For ages 11–16

"Trust but verify" is an old phrase, and it is the whole method of this site. It means: don't be a cynic who believes nothing, and don't be a pushover who believes everything — **believe things in proportion to the evidence, and go look at the evidence yourself.**

So every health page here comes with its workings on show:

- **How good the evidence is** — a badge on each page says plainly how strong or shaky the proof is, and the page names its own weakest point. A page that hid its weaknesses would be doing exactly what we criticise.
- **Which clock it's on** — another badge marks whether a risk is mostly a near-term one (the next ten years) or a lifetime one (the next seventy), because that changes how much it should matter to you right now.
- **The actual sources** — the studies are named so you can look them up. You don't have to; the point is that you *could*.

**Information is power** in a very literal sense: a lot of products are designed to be easy to start and hard to stop, and they work best on people who never check the evidence. Checking is the move that puts you back in charge.

## For ages 16+

The method, stated plainly. Every claim on this site is presented with three things attached: its **study design**, its **effect size**, and its **honest weakness**. Every figure is tracked in a public [provenance](/provenance/) ledger, checked against its source and linked so you can go and read the original for yourself. This is a reference, not a campaign: no calls to action, no donate button. If it ever reads as a nice-looking set of assertions, it has failed — a confident claim with no working shown is no better than the marketing it hopes to improve on.

"Trust but verify" also sets the register for how these topics are pitched at each age — reassurance where the real risk is low, straight talk where it isn't, and never a scary number without the honest context around it.

And here is the honest boundary of the whole enterprise, put where you can see it:

> This entire site assumes you **care what happens to a future version of you** — the you of ten, forty, seventy years from now. Almost every argument here is a trade: a small cost now for a larger benefit to that future person. If you genuinely do not care what happens to future-you, and you are *certain* your future self will never feel differently, then there is nothing for you here — the trade has no value if you place no value on the other side of it. We think that certainty is very hard to earn honestly, because people's future selves reliably turn out to care. But we are not going to pretend the assumption isn't there. It is the one thing this site cannot prove to you.

## Two different clocks

Most "living well" advice is about the **seventy-year clock**, not the **ten-year clock** — and the two lists barely overlap. For a teenager in England, the leading causes of death over the *next decade* are suicide and self-harm, injury (chiefly road), and cancer — not lung cancer, cirrhosis or heart disease. Deaths in this age group are rare, but their distribution is not what most of this advice addresses. It is worth knowing which clock a given claim is really about — each page here says so.

## The evidence

The "evidence" for this page is how the rest of the site is built:

- **Every claim links to its source.** Figures cite the study by name and year, and link straight to the paper, so you can read the original rather than take our word for it. Where it matters, the study design is named (randomised trial, cohort, Mendelian randomisation) — because the design is often the difference between a real finding and a confounded one.
- **A provenance page.** Every figure used on the site is gathered in one place — the [provenance](/provenance/) page — with its source, so nothing floats free of a citation.
- **Grading on every page.** Two badges on each page — how confident the claim is, and which clock it sits on — put the site's own assessment up front rather than burying it, so you can weigh a near-certain claim differently from a shaky one.

## The honest weakness

Two, stated in the site's own spirit. First, a linked citation only means we have quoted the paper faithfully — it does not mean the paper is correct; the underlying science can still be wrong, and several figures here carry real uncertainty flagged on their own pages. That is exactly why the link is there: so you can go and check. Second, and more fundamental: the whole project rests on the assumption above — that you value your future self. That is not a fact we can establish for you. It is the premise you bring, and everything else follows from it.
