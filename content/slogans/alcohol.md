## For ages 8–11

You are too young to drink, so this page is not really about tonight — it is about one honest, slightly boring fact worth knowing early.

Here it is: **alcohol is never actually *good* for your body.** Not in a small amount, not in a large one. You will hear people say things like "a glass of wine is good for your heart" — that turned out to be a mistake in how the old studies were done. There is no amount that *helps*.

That does not mean a single drink will badly hurt an adult — usually it won't. It just means there is no magic safe level where alcohol switches from good to bad, because it was never on the good side to begin with.

## For ages 11–16

Here is the thing the lifetime-cancer statistics miss: for someone your age, **the risk from alcohol is tonight, not thirty years away.** The real dangers are immediate and physical — injury, being hurt in a fight, drowning, choking, passing out, and things that get done to you or by you that cannot be taken back. That is a completely different, and much closer, kind of harm than liver disease decades from now.

One honest point to hold onto: **there is no amount of alcohol that is good for you** — the idea that "a glass of red wine is healthy" turned out to be a mistake in how the old studies were built. But be fair about size too: for an adult, the extra risk from *one* drink is small. The problem is the shape of the line, not usually the size of one drink.

What you do now bends the line later — but the more useful message for you is the near one: the danger you can actually see coming is same-night, not same-lifetime.

## For ages 16+

Here is the claim in full, with its honest limits. "No safe dose" is true, but it is a claim about **the shape of the curve, not the size of the risk** — and both halves matter, because the scary half on its own would overstate how large that risk is.

- The curve **rises from the very first drink**: there is no threshold below which risk is zero. The old "J-curve" that seemed to show light drinking protecting your heart was an artefact — the "non-drinkers" it compared against were contaminated with people who had *stopped* because they were already ill.
- **But the absolute risk of one drink a week is small.** There is also no *safe* dose of cycling, or sunlight, or getting into a car. So the honest formulation keeps the truth and drops the overclaim: *there is no dose at which alcohol is good for you — there never was — and the risk starts at your first drink and rises with every one after it.*

How today bends future risk: because the line has no threshold, risk accumulates with total lifetime intake rather than waiting for some "safe limit" to be crossed. The lever is the long-run pattern, not any single night — and the strongest honest illustration is that breast-cancer risk rises measurably per daily unit, with no floor (see the evidence).

## The evidence

- **Zhao, Stockwell, Naimi et al., [JAMA Netw Open 2023;6:e236185](<https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.6185>)** — 107 cohort studies, **4.8 million participants**. The apparent protection of low-volume drinking **disappeared** after adjustment for study characteristics, chiefly abstainer bias: reference "non-drinker" groups contaminated with sick-quitters and former heavy drinkers. No significant mortality reduction below 25 g/day; significantly increased mortality at **≥25 g/day in women and ≥45 g/day in men**. Apparent cardioprotection appeared only in older cohorts — precisely those most exposed to lifetime selection bias. (Corrected May 2023 for confidence-interval errors in the figure.)
- **Millwood et al., [Lancet 2019;393:1831](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(18)31772-0>)** (China Kadoorie Biobank, ~500,000 people) — the decisive study. Using *ALDH2* rs671 and *ADH1B* rs1229984 as genetic instruments, conventional epidemiology showed the familiar U-shape for stroke; the genetic analysis showed a **monotonic linear increase**. Genotype is assigned at conception and cannot be confounded by social position or sick-quitting. The U-shape was confounding.
- **IARC ([Monograph 100E](<https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK304380/>))** classifies alcoholic beverages as Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans) since 1988, and acetaldehyde associated with alcohol consumption as Group 1 — causal for cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, oesophagus, liver, colorectum and female breast.
- **Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, [Br J Cancer 2002](<https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.bjc.6600596>)** — breast cancer risk rises about **7% per 10 g/day** of alcohol, with no threshold. This is the strongest single support for "no safe dose": the curve rises from zero.
- **[UK Chief Medical Officers 2016](<https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/alcohol-consumption-advice-on-low-risk-drinking>)** guidance: no level of drinking is completely safe; keep to ≤14 units/week for both sexes. **Canada's Guidance on Alcohol and Health ([CCSA 2023](<https://www.ccsa.ca/canadas-guidance-alcohol-and-health>))** went further, abandoning thresholds for a continuum (1–2 drinks/week low risk, 3–6 moderate, 7+ increasingly high).

## The honest weakness

"No safe dose" is a claim about monotonicity, not magnitude. There is also no *safe* dose of cycling, of sunlight, or of getting into a car. The absolute excess risk at one drink a week is very small — so the honest version keeps the truth and drops the overclaim: **"There is no dose at which alcohol is good for you. There never was. The risk starts at your first drink and rises with every one after it."**
