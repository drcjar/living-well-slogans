## The claim

Narrowly: physical activity lowers mortality; the benefit is largest for people moving *out of the lowest* activity band; and the dose that captures most of it is far below the round numbers in circulation. The famous "10,000 steps" is not the target and never was a finding.

## The evidence

- **WHO 2020 guidelines (Bull et al., Br J Sports Med 2020;54:1451)** — 150–300 min/week of moderate activity for adults; 60 min/day for 5–17-year-olds. The 2020 revision removed the old 10-minute bout minimum: all movement counts.
- **Ekelund et al., BMJ 2019;366:l4570** — harmonised meta-analysis of accelerometer-measured cohorts (~36,000 adults), so no self-report inflation. The dose-response is steeply inverse and front-loaded: the largest mortality reduction comes from moving out of the lowest activity quartile. Getting off the floor matters far more than optimising the top.
- **Paluch et al., Lancet Public Health 2022;7:e219** — steps meta-analysis, ~47,000 adults. Mortality falls up to roughly **6,000–8,000 steps/day** in over-60s and **8,000–10,000 steps/day** in under-60s, then plateaus.
- **10,000 steps has no evidential parentage.** It comes from *manpo-kei* ("10,000-step meter"), the brand name of a pedometer sold by Yamasa around the 1964 Tokyo Olympics — chosen partly because the character 万 (10,000) resembles a walking figure. Use this: it is the friendliest possible demonstration that a number can circulate for sixty years as health advice without ever having been a finding.
- **Causality**: reverse causation is the obvious threat — ill people move less. Mendelian randomisation using accelerometer-derived phenotypes (Klimentidis et al., Eur Heart J 2018) supports a genuine protective effect, materially strengthening the observational picture.

## The honest weakness

The upper reaches of the curve are confounded beyond what adjustment fixes; people doing 300 min/week differ from the sedentary in ways questionnaires do not capture. The claim is strongest exactly where it matters most for a population — the bottom of the distribution — where the effect is largest and the Mendelian-randomisation support best. Do not oversell the top end.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

This is the encouraging one. You do not need a gym membership or 10,000 steps; the biggest single gain on the whole curve is simply not being in the least-active group. Whatever gets you off the sofa — walking, cycling, a sport you tolerate — captures most of the benefit, and there is no round number you have to hit.

## The slogan to attack

**"10,000 steps a day."** A 1960s pedometer brand name, not a dose derived from any study, now embedded in every fitness tracker as though it were medicine. Replace it with the honest version: move out of the bottom band, and everything after that is a bonus.
