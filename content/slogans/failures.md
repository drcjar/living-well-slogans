## The claim

Narrowly: the best-known "personal responsibility" health slogans have been evaluated and mostly do not work; and the genuine population-level wins came from structural measures — law, tax, product regulation — not from exhortation. The one honourable exception (Back to sleep) tells you exactly when a pure information campaign *can* work.

## The evidence

- **DARE / "Just say no"** — Ennett et al., Am J Public Health 1994;84:1394 (meta-analysis) and West & O'Neal, Am J Public Health 2004;94:1027 found effects on *knowledge* and essentially none on drug use; some prevention programmes show iatrogenic effects. The most heavily funded school drug-education programme in history did not change use.
- **"When the fun stops, stop"** — withdrawn 2021; no evidence of benefit and a signal of backfire (see [gambling](/slogans/gambling/)).
- **"Drink responsibly"** — no dose defined, written by the seller, blame pre-assigned (see [alcohol](/slogans/alcohol/)).
- **"10,000 steps"** — a 1960s pedometer brand name, not a finding (see [movement](/slogans/movement/)).
- **"5 a day"** — chosen for achievability in a 1991 campaign, not derived from a dose-response curve (see [food](/slogans/food/)).

## The honest weakness

Attributing a population change to *any* single cause is hard, and the counterfactual is never clean — the structural story below is a modelling inference, not a randomised trial of policy. What the record does support is a *relative* claim: where slogans and structural measures were both tried, the structural measures carry the population effect. That is enough for the argument, and no more than the evidence allows.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

What actually worked was not education — it was changing the environment, so the harmful choice stopped being the easy one:

- Adult smoking prevalence in Great Britain fell from roughly **45%** in the mid-1970s to about **11–12%** now. Tobacco-control modelling (SimSmoke and similar) attributes the great majority of that decline to taxation, advertising bans, smokefree legislation, plain packaging and age-of-sale law — and comparatively little to school health education.
- Seatbelt wearing did not rise because of "Clunk click"; it rose because of the 1983 law, which was associated with roughly a 25% reduction in driver deaths.
- **"Back to sleep" is the honourable outlier**: a pure information campaign that halved sudden infant death — and it worked because it asked for **one action, once, from a motivated actor, with no commercial product opposing it**. That is the exception that defines the rule.

The conclusion to give them: the point is *not* to resolve to be better — resolutions have a poor evidence base. The point is that some very clever, very well-funded people have designed products — cigarettes, drinks, slot apps, snack foods, feeds — to be hard to stop using, and have then written slogans telling you that stopping is your responsibility. You cannot out-willpower a design. But you can **see** it — and once you can, you get to choose your own defaults: what's in the cupboard, what's on your phone, who you're with, what time you go to bed.

## The slogan to attack

All of them on the *failed* side of the spine — but the sharpest single tell is the word "responsibly". When someone selling a product tells you to enjoy it responsibly, ask who benefits from that sentence. It defines no safe dose, and it decides in advance whose fault any harm will be.

> **Smoking kills — stopping helps.**
> **Alcohol: no safe dose.**
> **Gambling: designed so you lose.**
> **Move more, sit less. Sleep like it matters. Slip, slop, slap.**
> **And when someone tells you to enjoy their product responsibly — ask who benefits from that sentence.**
