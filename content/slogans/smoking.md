## The claim

Two claims, not one, and the second is what turns an epitaph into an intervention: that smoking causes death on a large scale, and that stopping — even late — recovers most of the loss. Stated narrowly, we are asserting causation for the first clause and reversibility for the second, each on its own evidence.

## The evidence

### Clause 1 — smoking kills

- **Doll & Hill, BMJ 1950;2:739** — hospital case-control, 649 lung cancer cases; the association was already unmissable.
- **British Doctors Study — Doll, Peto, Boreham & Sutherland, BMJ 2004;328:1519** — 34,439 male doctors, 50 years of follow-up, near-complete ascertainment. Cigarette smokers died on average **about 10 years younger** than never-smokers.
- **Pirie et al., Lancet 2013;381:133** (Million Women Study) and **Jha et al., NEJM 2013;368:341** (US NHIS) independently reproduce the ~10-year figure — different countries, sexes, eras.
- **Banks et al., BMC Med 2015** (Australian 45 and Up cohort) — roughly **two-thirds of lifelong smokers are killed by smoking**, an upward revision of the older "half" figure.
- **Mechanism is closed, not inferred**: Denissenko et al., Science 1996;274:430 showed benzo[a]pyrene diol epoxide forms adducts preferentially at the exact *TP53* codons (157, 248, 273) that are mutational hotspots in smokers' lung tumours. Exposure → adduct → mutation → tumour, at base-pair resolution.
- **Bradford Hill, Proc R Soc Med 1965;58:295** — the viewpoints paper on causal inference was written *about this exposure*.

### Clause 2 — stopping helps

- **Doll et al. 2004**: cessation at 60, 50, 40 and 30 gained approximately **3, 6, 9 and 10 years** of life expectancy respectively.
- **Pirie et al. 2013**: stopping before 40 avoided **>90%** of the excess mortality; stopping before 30 avoided **>97%**. The risk is not banked — it is largely reversible if you get out early.

Note the structure: a threat **plus an exit**. Threat without exit produces fatalism, which is why "smoking kills" alone is a worse slogan than the full line.

## The honest weakness

Essentially none on causation — this is the exception on the site where the honest weakness really is negligible. The live arguments are about policy, not about whether the claim is true. The one thing to be careful of in front of a 2026 audience is conflating cigarettes with vaping (below); the ~10-year deficit is a fact about combustion, and importing it wholesale onto e-cigarettes would be the kind of overclaim this whole project exists to avoid.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

Almost all lifetime smokers start before age 20. **Initiation is the only part of the pathway fully under your control** — and it is the single highest-leverage health decision available to you, because not starting removes the entire downstream problem at no cost.

On vaping, be straight, because they will know if you aren't:

- Nicotine vaping delivers far lower levels of the combustion toxicants that cause the ~10-year deficit. It is **not** equivalent to smoking, and pretending otherwise destroys your credibility for everything else.
- **Cochrane (Lindson et al., 2024)**: high-certainty evidence that nicotine e-cigarettes help *established smokers* quit, better than nicotine-replacement therapy. For a smoker, switching is a gain.
- For a never-smoker that argument does not apply at all: dependence acquired with no offsetting benefit, and 30-year safety data cannot exist for a product introduced around 2007.
- **EVALI (2019–20) was vitamin E acetate in illicit THC cartridges**, not nicotine vapes. Say so — getting this wrong is the fastest way to lose the room.

## The slogan to attack

There is no industry counterpart worth attacking here, which is itself the point: this is the one exposure where the public-health message won outright. The instructive comparison is historical — tobacco control moved from the hedged "smoking **may** damage your health" to the flat "smoking kills", and the population effect followed the plainer claim.
