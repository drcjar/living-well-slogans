## The claim

Two things at once. First, the slogan we are *retiring* — "eat less, move more" — is an identity, not an instruction: true in the way "spend less than you earn" is true, and just as unhelpful. Second, the replacement — Michael Pollan's "eat food, mostly plants, not too much" — is better because two of its three clauses are about *what to buy*, i.e. the food environment, and only the third concerns restraint. The honest header for this page is that the underlying evidence base is the weakest on the site, and we say so.

## The evidence

- **Hall et al., Cell Metab 2019;30:67** — the best-controlled feeding experiment in nutrition. Inpatient randomised crossover, 20 adults, ultra-processed versus unprocessed diets matched for calories, sugar, fat, fibre and macronutrients, eaten *ad libitum*. On the ultra-processed diet people spontaneously ate about **500 kcal/day** more and gained weight; they lost it on the unprocessed diet. The amount eaten was a property of the food, not of the person — the whole argument in one trial.
- **Hall et al., Obesity 2016;24:1612** (the "Biggest Loser" follow-up) — six years after extreme weight loss, resting metabolic rate remained suppressed beyond what weight loss alone predicted, and 13 of 14 participants regained. The body defends its mass; "eat less" is advice to fight a homeostatic system with conscious attention.
- The randomised-trial record for diet *composition* is genuinely poor. **Women's Health Initiative** low-fat diet: null for cardiovascular disease and breast cancer. **Look AHEAD (NEJM 2013)**: sustained weight loss in type 2 diabetes but null for cardiovascular events. **PREDIMED (Estruch et al., NEJM 2018)** is the strongest positive trial (about a 30% relative reduction in major cardiovascular events) — and it had to be retracted and republished after randomisation irregularities.
- **GBD 2017 Diet Collaborators, Lancet 2019;393:1958** — roughly **11 million deaths per year** attributable to dietary risks, dominated by high sodium, low whole grains and low fruit. The top three are about what is absent or added, not about total calories.
- **"5 a day" is a marketing number, not an evidence threshold** — chosen in a 1991 US campaign for achievability. Dose-response meta-analysis (Aune et al., Int J Epidemiol 2017) suggests benefit continuing to about **800 g/day** of fruit and vegetables, i.e. roughly ten portions. A clean, non-cynical example of a number chosen for tractability and later mistaken for a finding.

## The honest weakness

State it plainly: nutritional epidemiology is the weakest field represented on this site. Self-reported food-frequency questionnaires, massive residual confounding by socioeconomic position, and a replication record that would embarrass any other discipline. The Hall crossover is the honourable exception, and it is small (n=20, four weeks). Anything stronger than "the food environment is engineered for overconsumption, and this has been shown experimentally once, well" is overclaiming — and this page's `confidence` is set to *moderate* for exactly that reason.

## What this means for a 15-year-old

The lever you actually control is not willpower at the plate; it is what is in the cupboard and what is easiest to reach. The Hall trial says the quantity you eat is partly set by how processed the food is — so the useful move is upstream of hunger: change the defaults in the kitchen rather than trying to out-argue your appetite three times a day.

## The slogan to attack

**"Eat less, move more."** It contains no information about *why* intake is high and silently locates the cause in the eater's character — the obesity equivalent of "just say no". Retire it in favour of a rule about what to buy.
